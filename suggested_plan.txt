Below is a 30-minute seminar plan organized as a 12-slide talk. It is designed for a master’s-level ML audience that already knows standard optimization, backprop, transformers/CNNs, and basic generalization concepts. The plan treats the two papers as a single narrative arc:
•	Paper 1: Instance-dependent Early Stopping (IES) — stop training at the sample level using second-order loss differences.
•	Paper 2: GradES — stop training at the transformer weight-matrix/component level using gradient-change magnitudes, avoiding validation-based stopping overhead.

A good seminar framing is:

“Early stopping is usually global. These papers ask: what if stopping decisions were made at finer granularity — per instance or per component?”

•	Slides: 12

Slide 2 — Problem setup: why global early stopping is too coarse

What to say
•	Classical early stopping answers only: “Should the whole model stop now?”
•	But learning is heterogeneous:
•	some training examples become easy earlier than others
•	some transformer submodules stabilize earlier than others
•	Therefore, global stopping can waste compute in two ways:
1.	keep backpropagating through already-mastered samples
2.	keep updating already-converged matrices while waiting for slower parts to catch up
•	This slide should unify the two papers conceptually before diving into details.

Figure to attach
•	Prefer a custom comparison cartoon:
•	left: one stop button for the whole model
•	middle: some dataset points greyed out
•	right: some transformer blocks locked/frozen
•	Optional small caption below: “Both papers replace one global stop signal with many local stop decisions.”

⸻

Slide 3 — Paper 1 motivation: Instance-dependent Early Stopping (IES)

What to say
•	IES starts from the observation that different instances are learned at different speeds.
•	The paper’s key principle is:
Once the model has mastered an instance, training on it should stop.
•	Important distinction: this is not dataset pruning before training and not a fixed sampling schedule.
•	It is a dynamic during-training mechanism that removes instances from backpropagation once they are judged mastered.
•	Mention the empirical headline:
•	saves roughly 10%–55% mini-batches
•	gives around 1.2×–1.4× wall-time speedup
•	while keeping comparable accuracy across CIFAR/ImageNet settings.

Figure to attach
•	Paper 1, Figure 1 (page 2): top row accuracy vs epoch; bottom row excluded samples over training.
This is the best “why care?” figure because it visually shows:
•	performance stays similar
•	excluded samples increase over time.

⸻

Slide 4 — IES method: what does “mastered” mean?

What to say
•	Explain the design problem:
•	using raw loss L_i directly is not ideal because different samples can have different optimal losses due to ambiguity, noise, imbalance, or intrinsic difficulty.
•	The paper’s solution:
•	use the second-order difference of the per-sample loss across epochs
•	intuition: a mastered sample is not necessarily low-loss, but its loss becomes locally flat / insensitive to updates
•	State the core quantity:
•	\Delta^2 L_i^{(t)} = L_i^{(t)} - 2L_i^{(t-1)} + L_i^{(t-2)}
•	Explain why this helps:
•	they argue higher-order loss differences have lower dispersion late in training, so one can use a single threshold \delta for all samples.

Figure to attach
•	Paper 1, Figure 3 (page 5): coefficient of variation of loss differences.
This is the right figure for justifying the use of second-order differences instead of raw loss.

Presenter note
•	This is a mathematically important slide. Spend time on intuition, not only formula.

⸻

Slide 5 — IES algorithm and mechanism

What to say
•	Walk through the algorithm in plain language:
1.	forward pass on all data to compute per-sample losses
2.	compute second-order loss differences
3.	mark mastered samples
4.	exclude mastered samples from backpropagation
5.	continue until conventional stopping or everything is mastered
•	Important nuance:
•	forward still runs on all samples
•	the savings come mainly from reduced backprop, not from skipping all computation
•	State the mechanism claim:
•	by focusing optimization on not-yet-mastered samples, the remaining batch tends to contain samples with larger gradient norms
•	this can accelerate training progress and reduce sharpness more quickly.

Figure to attach
•	Paper 1, Algorithm 1 (page 6) as a cropped figure.
•	Optionally add a small custom pipeline diagram next to it:
•	forward on all → mastery test → filter → backprop on subset.

Slide 6 — IES empirical results: what matters most

What to say
•	Focus on the strongest empirical claims:
•	across architectures and optimizers on CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100, IES keeps accuracy close to baseline while saving substantial mini-batches
•	on ImageNet-1k it also preserves performance with about ~1.3× speedup
•	pretrained models using IES can improve downstream transfer performance on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Caltech-101
•	Mention transfer result because it makes the paper stronger than a pure efficiency paper:
•	the authors report average transfer gains around 1.5% after ImageNet-1k pretraining.

Slide 7 — Paper 2 motivation: GradES for transformers

What to say
•	Transition:
•	IES refined stopping at the data/sample level.
•	GradES refines stopping at the model/component level.
•	Key motivation:
•	in large transformers, classical early stopping is expensive because it requires repeated validation inference
•	different transformer matrices converge at different rates
•	GradES uses gradient information already produced during training, so it avoids the extra validation-cost bottleneck.
•	Mention scope:
•	tested on language models and vision-language models
•	with both full fine-tuning and LoRA.

Figure to attach
•	Paper 2, Figure 1 (page 2): gradient-magnitude curves for different matrices in a transformer layer.
This is the central motivation figure because it visually shows heterogeneous convergence across components.

Slide 8 — GradES method: matrix-level convergence detection

What to say
•	Explain what is monitored:
•	for each transformer matrix W (e.g. W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, W_{up}, W_{down}, W_{gate})
•	track the L1 magnitude of gradient changes
•	Stopping rule:
•	after a grace period \alpha T, if gradient-change magnitude drops below threshold \tau, freeze that matrix
•	Important design choice:
•	frozen matrices still allow gradient flow through them
•	they are not updated, but they remain in the computation graph
•	Explain why the grace period matters:
•	without it, gradients are initially small and many components would freeze too early.

Figure to attach
•	Paper 2, Figure 2 (page 3): architecture diagram showing locked components.
•	Optional side box with the key algorithmic steps in your own words.

Slide 9 — GradES results: accuracy, speed, and LoRA interaction

What to say
•	Core empirical message:
•	GradES gets 1.57×–7.22× speedup
•	while maintaining or slightly improving accuracy on language and multimodal tasks.
•	Highlight the most memorable example:
•	on Qwen3-0.6B, LoRA+GradES trains in 907 s vs 6550 s for standard fine-tuning, with slightly better accuracy.
•	Stress the contrast with classical early stopping:
•	validation-based early stopping may actually increase runtime because of validation overhead
•	GradES avoids that by using training-time gradients instead.
•	Mention that LoRA seems especially compatible because reduced parameter space may produce clearer convergence signals.

Slide 10 — Direct comparison: IES vs GradES

What to say
This slide should be the conceptual center of the seminar.

Comparison dimensions to cover
•	Granularity
•	IES: sample-level
•	GradES: parameter-matrix/component-level
•	Signal used
•	IES: second-order differences of per-sample loss
•	GradES: gradient-change magnitude per matrix
•	Where savings come from
•	IES: fewer samples in backprop
•	GradES: fewer parameter updates + no validation overhead
•	Model family
•	IES: general deep nets, demonstrated on CNNs and even detection/segmentation setups
•	GradES: specifically transformer/VLM fine-tuning
•	Threshold challenge
•	IES uses \delta
•	GradES uses \tau plus grace period \alpha
•	Implicit regularization story
•	IES: focus on harder/unmastered samples, larger gradients, flatter minima
•	GradES: freeze fast-converged submodules, prevent overfitting there while slower parts continue learning.

Figure to attach
•	Use a custom comparison table rather than a paper figure.
•	Make it clean and seminar-friendly.

Suggested closing line for this slide

“IES asks which data points still deserve gradient updates; GradES asks which model components still deserve gradient updates.”

Slide 11 — Critical assessment: strengths, assumptions, and open questions

What to say
This slide should show academic maturity.

For IES
•	Strengths:
•	elegant idea
•	model-agnostic flavor
•	good empirical breadth
•	transfer improvements make the story stronger
•	Open questions / limitations:
•	no full theory for why the second-order loss difference is the best criterion
•	fairness impact is not thoroughly studied
•	forward pass on all samples is still required, so the method does not eliminate all overhead.

For GradES
•	Strengths:
•	very practical for large transformers
•	avoids validation overhead
•	integrates with LoRA
•	includes some convergence theory
•	Limitations:
•	requires manual threshold tuning \tau
•	monitoring adds ~3% overhead
•	static freezing may freeze too early because it lacks a patience-style mechanism
•	unclear generalization to non-transformer architectures.

Broader critique
•	Both papers rely on a local proxy for “done-ness”
•	A major research question is whether these local signals can be made adaptive, theoretically grounded, and hyperparameter-light

Figure to attach
•	No paper figure needed.
•	Use a two-column pros / caveats slide.

Slide 12 — Conclusion and discussion prompts

What to say
End by synthesizing the lessons.

Main conclusion
•	Fine-grained early stopping is a promising direction for efficient training.
•	The two papers show that stopping can be moved:
•	from global → per instance
•	from global → per transformer component
•	The shared theme is:
Training effort should follow where learning is still happening.

Final takeaways
1.	Global early stopping is too coarse for heterogeneous learning dynamics.
2.	Local stopping criteria can save compute while preserving or improving performance.
3.	The most interesting future work is likely hybrid stopping:
•	per-sample + per-component
•	adaptive thresholds
•	theory connecting stopping proxies to generalization

Good seminar discussion questions
•	Could IES and GradES be combined in one pipeline?
•	Can thresholds \delta and \tau be learned online?
•	How robust are these methods under label noise, distribution shift, or class imbalance?
•	Are there settings where fine-grained freezing hurts fairness or minority performance?
