Fine-Grained Early Stopping
in Deep Learning

Hubert Michalski • ML Seminar • March 2026

Welcome. Today we look at two recent papers that challenge the coarseness of traditional early stopping. Both ask: can we make finer-grained stopping decisions during training? We'll cover the problem framing, then dive into each paper, and finish with a comparison and critique. ~30 minutes total, with time for discussion at the end.

Why Global Early Stopping Is Too Coarse

Classical ES answers one question:

"Has the entire model stopped improving on the entire validation set?"

One signal → one decision → all parameters, all data

Two types of wasted compute:

  • IES Backpropagating through already-mastered samples — the model keeps training on data it has nothing more to learn from
  • GradES Updating already-converged weight matrices — some transformer components finish learning long before others

Both papers replace one global stop signal with many local stop decisions

Classical early stopping is a blunt instrument. It monitors one metric (validation loss) and makes one binary decision for the entire model. These two papers identify two distinct sources of wasted computation that global ES misses. IES focuses on the data dimension, GradES on the model dimension.

IES Motivation

Yuan, Lin, Feng, Han, Liu — "Instance-Dependent Early Stopping" (ICLR 2025)

  • Instances are learned at different speeds during training
  • Dynamic removal of mastered samples from backpropagation (not dataset pruning — removal happens during training)
  • Forward pass still computed on all samples; savings come from skipping backprop

10–55% mini-batch savings, 1.2–1.4× wall-time speedup

Fig 1: Accuracy maintained while excluded samples grow over epochs

This paper is published at ICLR 2025. The key insight is simple: not all training samples need the same number of gradient updates. Look at the figure — as training progresses, more and more samples are identified as mastered and excluded, yet accuracy stays the same or even improves. Importantly, this is NOT dataset pruning. Pruning selects a subset before training. IES dynamically removes samples during training as they become mastered.

IES What "Mastered" Means

Problem: Raw loss is unsuitable — different samples have different optimal loss values \(L_i(w^*)\)

Second-order loss difference:

\[\Delta^2 L_i(w^{(t)}) = L_i(w^{(t)}) - 2L_i(w^{(t-1)}) + L_i(w^{(t-2)})\]
  • Intuition: Measures the curvature of the loss trajectory, not its level
  • Flat loss ≠ low loss; what matters is no more change
  • Lower CV than 0th/1st order → single threshold \(\delta\) works across all samples

Mastered criterion: \(|\Delta^2 L_i(w^{(t')})| < \delta\) for recent epochs

Fig 3: CV of loss differences by order — 2nd order has lowest CV

This is the key math slide — spend time on intuition. Why not just use the loss value directly? Because different samples have different optimal losses due to noise, complexity, class imbalance. A noisy sample might have high loss even when fully learned. The second-order difference captures whether the loss is CHANGING, not its absolute value. The CV plot is crucial: it shows that second-order differences have much lower variance across samples, meaning one threshold delta works for all.

IES Algorithm

  1. Forward pass on all samples — compute \(L_i(w^{(t)})\) for every \(i \in \mathcal{D}\)
  2. Compute second-order differences \(\Delta^2 L_i\) for each sample
  3. Identify mastered: if \(|\Delta^2 L_i| < \delta\), mark sample as mastered
  4. Update training set: remove mastered samples from backprop batch
  5. Backpropagate only on remaining (non-mastered) samples

Key nuance: Forward pass is still on ALL samples (needed to compute \(\Delta^2 L_i\)). Savings come from reduced backpropagation only.

Algorithm 1: Instance-dependent Early Stopping

Walk through the algorithm step by step. Important: the forward pass is still on all samples — we need the loss values to decide who is mastered. The savings come from not computing gradients and not doing backprop on mastered samples. This means the speedup is bounded by the ratio of backprop cost to total cost. Also note: conventional early stopping on validation set is still used as a fallback (line 7).

IES Why Does It Help?

Removing mastered instances doesn't just save compute — it accelerates optimization:

  • Larger gradient norms — remaining batch has more informative gradients
  • Faster loss reduction — effective learning rate increases on hard samples
  • Flatter minima — lower SAM values and Hessian eigenvalues → better generalization

Fig 4: Test error, gradient norm, SAM value, and Hessian eigenvalue — IES (orange) vs baseline (blue)

This is the mechanistic explanation for why IES doesn't just match the baseline but can actually exceed it. By focusing backpropagation on not-yet-mastered samples, the gradient signal is more informative. The SAM and Hessian plots show IES finds flatter minima, which explains the transfer learning improvements.

GradES Motivation

Wen, Zeng, Zhou, Liu, Hosseinzadeh, Su, Rawassizadeh — "GradES: Significantly Faster Training in Transformers with Gradient-Based Early Stopping" (2025)

Transition: IES = data-level granularity. GradES = model-level granularity.

  • Different weight matrices in transformers converge at different rates
  • Uses gradient information already computed during backprop — no extra cost
  • No validation overhead — classical ES requires frequent validation passes that can paradoxically slow training

1.57–7.22× speedup, +1.2% avg accuracy (language), +3.88% (multimodal)

Fig 1: L1 norms of gradient changes per component in Qwen3-0.6B layer 7 — vastly different convergence rates

Now we shift from the data dimension to the model dimension. GradES observes that different weight matrices in a transformer converge at very different rates. Look at the figure: some components' gradients become tiny early on, while others keep changing. A key practical advantage: classical ES actually SLOWS training on large models because validation passes are expensive. GradES avoids this entirely.

GradES Method

Gradient change magnitude (element-wise L1):

\[G_W^{(l)}(t) = \left\| \nabla W_t^{(l)} - \nabla W_{t-1}^{(l)} \right\|_1\]
  • Grace period: Wait \(\alpha T\) steps before monitoring (let training stabilize)
  • Freezing rule: If \(G_W^{(l)}(t) < \tau\), freeze matrix \(W^{(l)}\) — skip weight updates
  • Key design: Frozen = no updates, but still in computation graph (gradient flow preserved for other layers)
  • Monitored per layer: \(W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, W_{up}, W_{down}, W_{gate}\)
  • Termination: When all matrices frozen → stop training

Fig 2: GradES architecture — lock icons show frozen components

The method is elegant: track how much each weight matrix's gradient changes between steps. If the gradient is barely changing, the matrix has converged — freeze it. Crucially, frozen matrices still participate in forward and backward passes — only the weight update is skipped. This preserves gradient flow to other layers, unlike pruning which would break the computation graph. The grace period alpha*T prevents premature freezing during the initial unstable phase of training.

IES vs GradES: Comparison

Dimension IES Instance-Dependent ES GradES Gradient-Based ES
Granularity Per training sample Per weight matrix
Signal 2nd-order loss differences \(\Delta^2 L_i\) L1 norm of gradient changes \(G_W\)
Savings source Skip backprop on mastered samples Skip weight updates for converged matrices
Model family CNNs (ResNet, VGG, DenseNet) Transformers (LLMs, VLMs)
Threshold Single \(\delta\) (justified by low CV) \(\tau\) per model (manual tuning)
Regularization Flatter minima → better transfer Prevents component-level overfitting

"IES asks which data points still deserve gradient updates;
GradES asks which model components still deserve gradient updates."

This is the conceptual center of the talk. Both papers share the same philosophical insight: training effort should be directed where learning is still happening. They just apply this insight along different axes — data vs model architecture. Note they're also complementary in model family: IES is demonstrated on CNNs, GradES on transformers.

Critical Assessment

IES

Strengths

  • Elegant & model-agnostic (any architecture/optimizer)
  • Improved transfer learning — unexpected bonus
  • Theoretically motivated (CV analysis)

Limitations

  • No theory for why 2nd-order is optimal
  • Forward pass still required on all samples
  • Fairness impact not studied

GradES

Strengths

  • Practical for large transformers (no validation overhead)
  • LoRA compatible — compounding efficiency
  • Convergence theory provided

Limitations

  • Manual \(\tau\) tuning per model (~3% overhead)
  • Static freezing — no mechanism to unfreeze
  • Unclear applicability beyond transformers
Let's be critical about both papers. IES is elegant but the choice of second-order differences is empirically motivated — they show it works better than 0th or 1st order, but don't prove it's optimal. The forward pass bottleneck is a real limitation: you still need to evaluate every sample every epoch. For GradES, the manual threshold tuning is a practical concern. The static freezing (no unfreeze) could be problematic if a component needs to adapt later in training.

Conclusion & Discussion

Training effort should follow where learning is still happening

Key Takeaways

  1. Global early stopping is too coarse — it ignores heterogeneity in both data and model components
  2. Local stopping criteria (per-sample or per-matrix) save significant compute while maintaining or improving accuracy
  3. Future direction: hybrid stopping combining data-level and component-level granularity

Discussion Questions

  • Can IES and GradES be combined? (per-sample × per-component stopping)
  • Can thresholds \(\delta, \tau\) be learned online instead of set manually?
  • How robust are these methods to label noise or distribution shift?
  • Fairness implications of excluding samples early?
To wrap up: both papers share a common insight that training resources should be directed where they're most needed. The natural next step is combining both approaches — stop training on mastered samples AND stop updating converged components. Open questions around threshold learning, robustness, and fairness provide plenty of research directions.
